Regolith

The lunar regolith is the uppermost layer of fine-grained material on the surface of the moon. It records billions of years of our solar system's history on its surface. It also harbors valuable resources like hydrogen and even water-ice in its subsurface. Understanding the thickness of the regolith allows us to constrain the depth at which these resources might be stable. It can additionally tell us where the ground might be littered with large boulders that are potential hazards for future landings. (left) Apollo 17 astronaut by Tracy's Rock. Credit: Apollo 17 Preliminary Science Report.

Consolidated rock has a higher thermal inertia than fine-grained regolith, so Diviner surface temperatures can tell us about rock distribution on the lunar surface. In my work , I calculate regolith thickness statistically, using these rock abundance estimates at cold spot craters which are visibly fresh impact craters surrounded by a large region of reduced nighttime temperatures. The main idea is as follows: small impact craters will excavate only regolith while larger craters will excavate more consolidated rock detectable by Diviner (image above). We can therefore measure lunar regolith thickness as the depth at which these craters excavate consolidated rock detectable by Diviner.

We quantify depth using models of excavation depth from Melosh (1989) (image right). If half the craters in a particular diameter bin are rocky (rockiness fraction ​= ​0.5), we consider this to be an indication of rock excavation detectable by Diviner. Our results show that 50% of craters are rocky at a diameter of around 123 ​m in the mare, and 141 ​m in the highlands. These correspond to excavation depths of around 10 and 12 ​m respectively. Lunar mare craters seem, on average, to become rocky at slightly shallower depths than those on the highlands.
